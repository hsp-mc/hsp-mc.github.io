Instructor Overview: This document maps the SPHERE Thermal Suit experiment to standard European secondary physics frameworks. It includes lesson timings, safety protocols, expected analytical constants, and suggestions for discussion to streamline execution in single-session classroom windows.

1. CURRICULAR ALIGNMENT & LESSON SCHEME

This curriculum is aligned with the Inquiry-Based Science Education (IBSE) pedagogy, specifically mimicking the virtual-physical calibration workflows of the European Union Horizon 2020 SPHERE initiative. It directly maps to secondary physics curriculum segments covering:

  • Thermodynamics: Thermal conduction, convection, radiation, thermal barriers, and specific heat capacity calculations.
  • Mathematical Modeling: Rate equations, Newtonian decay curves, and parametric modeling.
  • Digital Twin Concepts: Students use a simplified computer model to compare predicted and measured results.

Suggested Lesson Structure (45-60 Minutes):

TIMING INQUIRY STEP STUDENT ACTIVITIES & MILESTONES
00 - 10 Min Engage & Certify Students review the deep-space context, read the student briefing, and complete the Pre-Flight Quiz to unlock mission telemetry.
10 - 20 Min Explore & Construct Student crews construct one of four thermal protection systems:
  • Bare Capsule
  • Cotton Layer
  • Mylar Layer
  • Mylar + Cotton + Bubble Wrap
They then install the temperature probe through the PG7 cable gland and prepare the capsule for testing.
20 - 25 Min Model & Predict Crews open the Simulator tab, select their insulation schemes, configure parameters (T₀ = 80°C), and execute mathematical predictions.
25 - 40 Min Explain & Measure Pour 80°C water into PET jars. Submerge in the 0°C ice baths. Start stopwatch. Students log temperatures every 60 seconds.
40 - 45 Min Elaborate & Grade Analyze the resulting Thermal Correlation Score. Discuss convective leaks, and record grading coefficients.

2. RISK ASSESSMENT & HAZARD MITIGATION

Enforce these standard safety mandates to guarantee zero accident rates during live laboratory sessions:

HAZARD LEVEL MANDATED MITIGATION STEPS
Hot Water Scalding HIGH Do not allow students to use water > 80°C. Teachers should pre-boil and dispense water pre-mixed with cold tap water to lock thermal limits at exactly 80°C.
Melted Ice Slippages MEDIUM Place physical ice baths in high-density plastic trays. Provide moisture absorbent towels. Keep ice bath tables completely segregated from laptops and tablets.
Electrical Shock MEDIUM Only permit low-voltage, USB-powered or battery-powered digital sensor readouts. Keep the thermocouple cables long to maximize distance from the computer screen.
Capsule Deformation LOW Ensure thin PET food jars are not exposed to boiling 100°C temperatures which causes polymer warping. Enforce the strict 80°C limit.
Summer & High-Ambient Temperature Adjustments (~30°C):
High ambient temperatures in summer classrooms can accelerate ice-bath degradation and introduce safety risks. Enforce these pedagogical workarounds:
  1. Thermal Baseline Safety Scaling: If ambient room temperatures are high, reduce the hot water target from 80°C to 55°C–60°C. This eliminates scalding risks and thermal stress on PET caps while preserving perfect exponential Newtonian cooling curves. Students simply adjust their initial temperature parameter (T0 = 60°C) inside the digital twin simulator to maintain flawless correlation.
  2. Ice Bath Melt Compensation: In hot rooms, the ice bath temperature may rise to 3°C–6°C due to melting. Instruct students to measure the actual bath temperature with their probe at the start of the re-entry drop, and configure the environmental temperature (Tenv) in both the simulator and chart overlays to match this observed value (e.g. Tenv= 4.5°C).
  3. Ice-Free Alternative Setup: If ice is logistically unavailable, students can use ambient room tap water (typically ~25°C–30°C) as their cooling environment. To simulate deep-space decay, configure the digital twin simulator with Tenv = 30°C (or the measured tap water temperature) and T0 = 60°C. The mathematical model behaves identically, allowing valid heat transfer comparisons.

Important Experimental Design Note:
Students do not test bubble wrap as a standalone insulation system. Bubble wrap is only used as an outer protective layer in the combined configuration (Mylar + Cotton + Bubble Wrap). This reflects real engineering practice, where multiple materials are combined to reduce different heat-transfer mechanisms simultaneously.

Expected Cooling Rates after 15-Minute Submersion:

4. CLASSROOM DISCUSSION & REFLECTION

The goal is to develop a scientific understanding and engineering intuition rather than achieving specific results. Teachers are encouraged to let the students discuss and assess their results using the questions below:

  • Which type of insulation performed best and why?
  • Did your measured temperatures match the simulated prediction? If not, what might explain the differences?
  • Which heat transfer mechanisms are dominant in which material?
  • How does this experiment differ from the actual conditions in space?
  • What limitations does this experimental model have?
  • How could the experiment be improved?
Teacher Note: Deviations between prediction and measurement should be viewed as opportunities for scientific discussion rather than as mistakes. Real experiments rarely match theoretical models perfectly.

Experiment Limitations

This experiment is a simplified model of thermal protection in space and does not perfectly represent real astronaut conditions.

  • Real space is a vacuum, while this experiment takes place in air and water.
  • Heat transfer in space is dominated by radiation, whereas conduction and convection are significant in this experiment.
  • Real spacesuits use many specialized insulation layers and active thermal control systems.
  • Astronauts continuously generate heat through metabolism.
  • The experiment is designed to illustrate heat transfer concepts rather than accurately reproduce space conditions.
