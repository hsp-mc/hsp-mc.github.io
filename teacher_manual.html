1. PEDAGOGICAL CONTEXT & SYLLABUS ALIGNMENT

The SPHERE: Mission Control Digital Twin Laboratory is an Inquiry-Based Science Education (IBSE) learning framework. It bridges physical thermal experiments with live mathematical simulation, designed specifically for secondary and upper-secondary physics and engineering curricula.

Target Learning Outcomes:

  • Thermodynamic Mechanisms: Differentiate between conduction, convection, and radiative heat transfer in complex insulating structures.
  • Newtonian Mathematical Modeling: Apply Newton's Law of Cooling $T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{env}})e^{-kt}$ to predict exponential decay.
  • Digital Twin Methodology: Evaluate discrepancies between physical sensor telemetry and computer models.
  • Data Literacy & Graphing: Manually log data points, plot continuous curves on grid paper templates, and calculate percentage correlation scores.

4. QUESTION SHEET SOLUTION & GRADING GUIDE

Q1 Answer (Mylar Mechanism):

Mylar has a highly reflective metallic coating that reflects infrared radiation (electromagnetic waves). In a vacuum, radiation is the dominant form of heat transfer. However, in an ice-water bath, conduction through direct liquid contact is extremely high. Mylar alone is thin and has high thermal conductivity, so it requires cotton or bubble wrap to block conductive heat transfer.

Q2 Answer (Decay Constant k):

A smaller $k$ value indicates a better thermal insulator. The term $-k \cdot t$ in the exponent determines how quickly temperature drops. Smaller $k$ values mean a slower decay rate, retaining heat inside the capsule for much longer.

Q3 Answer (MLI Combined Rationale):

MLI combines multiple distinct layers: cotton traps stationary air to minimize conduction, bubble wrap creates large sealed air pockets suppressing convection, and Mylar reflects thermal radiation. By targeting all three heat transfer pathways simultaneously, MLI achieves superior performance compared to any single material.

Q4 Answer (Physical vs Digital Twin Error Sources):

Acceptable answers include: (1) Sensor probe accidentally touching the jar wall instead of remaining centered; (2) Incomplete waterproofing causing cold water intrusion; (3) Ice bath warming slightly above 0°C; (4) Non-uniform water mixing inside the jar creating temperature gradients.

Q5 Answer (Summer Classroom Adaptation):

Students should measure the actual temperature of the ice bath using their thermometer and enter that value (e.g. 4.5°C) into the $T_{\text{env}}$ input field in Mission Control. This ensures the digital twin math model uses the exact physical boundary condition of the room.
