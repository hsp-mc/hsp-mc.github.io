WELCOME TO THE SPHERE THERMAL CHALLENGE

In this experiment, you will investigate how insulation helps to keep an object warm. Using a small water-filled capsule, you will compare different insulating materials and observe how temperature changes over time.

Astronauts need thermal protection because space contains extreme temperature differences. Modern spacesuits use several insulation layers to reduce heat loss and maintain a safe internal temperature.

Your capsule represents a simplified model of a spacesuit. The warm water inside represents the astronaut, while the surrounding ice-water bath represents a cold environment.

Before performing the experiment, you will use a mathematical model to predict how quickly the capsule cools down. You will then compare your prediction with real measurements.

Your goals: Understand heat transfer, build an insulated capsule, predict cooling behaviour, measure real temperatures, and compare theory with experiment.

UNDERSTANDING HEAT TRANSFER CORE SCIENCE

Heat can move from a warmer object to a colder object in three main ways. Understanding these processes helps explain why insulation works.

1. CONDUCTION

Conduction is heat transfer through direct contact.

Example: A metal spoon becomes hot when left inside a cup of tea.

In this experiment, cotton helps reduce conduction because it traps air, which is a poor conductor of heat.

2. CONVECTION

Convection occurs when liquids or gases move and carry heat with them.

Example: Warm air rises above a radiator.

Bubble wrap traps air in small pockets and reduces air movement, helping to reduce convection.

3. RADIATION

Radiation transfers heat through electromagnetic waves.

Example: You can feel warmth from the Sun even through empty space.

Reflective Mylar foil reduces radiative heat loss by reflecting infrared radiation.

THE CORE SCIENCE OF SPACE SUIT ANALOGS

COTTON SHEETS — REDUCING CONDUCTION

Mechanism: Cotton contains many small air pockets. Because air is a poor conductor of heat, cotton slows the transfer of thermal energy from the warm container to the colder surroundings.

BUBBLE WRAP — REDUCING CONVECTION

Mechanism: Bubble wrap traps air inside sealed pockets. Because the air cannot move freely, convection currents are reduced and heat is retained more effectively.

MYLAR FOIL — REDUCING RADIATION

Mechanism: Mylar is a reflective material that reduces heat transfer by radiation. Real spacecraft often use Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI), which consists of many reflective layers separated by small gaps. In this experiment, Mylar represents one part of that insulation system by reflecting infrared radiation.

CRITICAL CLASSROOM SAFETY PROTOCOLS

Thermodynamic experiments in school environments introduce manageable risks. Ensure the following rules are strictly enforced during the lab drop:

HAZARD DESCRIPTION RISK LAYER MITIGATION MANDATE
Boiling Water Scalding
Using boiling 100°C water directly in school environments.		 HIGH Enforce a strict 80°C limit. Provide thermal kettles pre-programmed to 80°C or use standard thermometers to lock temperatures before filling plastic capsules.
Ice-Bath Spillages
Slips/trips from condensation, melted ice, or water spills around electricity.		 MEDIUM Establish a clear separation between the ice water reservoirs and any tablet or laptop charting stations. Provide absorbent fiber cloths.
Capsule Leakage
PET plastic deformation if filled with temperatures > 90°C. 		LOW Never pour boiling water directly into thin plastic PET containers. Let water settle to 80°C, and securely lock the digital wire thread to prevent hot leaks.
SUMMER/HIGH-AMBIENT PROTOCOL (~30°C)

• Reduce Target Temp: Safety scale water down to 55°C–60°C to neutralize scalding risks on hot days. The simulator supports any initial value.
• Melt Compensation: Instruct students to measure actual bath temperature and input it as Tenv in the simulator if ice melts above 0°C.
• Ice-Free Option: Drop capsules in room-temperature tap water (~25°C–30°C) and configure Tenv accordingly.

BOARDWORK KEYFRAMES & EQUATIONS MATH_THEORY // DIRECTIVE

Pedagogical Guidance

Use these explanations to anchor class theories on the chalkboard:

1. SPECIFIC HEAT CAPACITY

Calculates total energy loss from internal core volume to surroundings:

Q = m · c · ΔT

Where: m = 0.1 kg (100ml water), c = 4184 J/kg·°C (Specific heat capacity of water), ΔT = Temperature change. This equation calculates the amount of thermal energy gained or lost by water when its temperature changes.

2. NEWTON'S LAW OF COOLING

Models the rate of heat loss proportional to temperature differences:

T(t) = Tenv + (T₀ - Tenv) · e-kt

Where: T₀ = 80°C, Tenv = 0°C (ice bath), k = insulation cooling constant, t = time. For this project, students do not need to derive the equation. The model is used to explore how insulation affects cooling behaviour and how mathematical predictions compare with experimental data.

INTERACTIVE PRACTICE: HOW TO CALCULATE YOUR DECAY CONSTANT (k)

STEP-BY-STEP MATH TOOL

The decay constant k measures how fast heat leaks out of your capsule. You can calculate your capsule's exact k-value from your lab thermometer readings using this simple formula derived from Newton's Law of Cooling:

k = - [ ln( (T(t) - Tenv) / (T₀ - Tenv) ) ] / t
CALCULATED RESULTS & STEP-BY-STEP BREAKDOWN:
1. Temp Ratio = (23.8 - 0) / (80.0 - 0) = 0.2975
2. Natural Log ln(0.2975) = -1.2123
3. DERIVED k-VALUE = 0.081 min⁻¹
MATCHING MATERIAL: COTTON SHIELD (k ≈ 0.080)

PRACTICE PROBLEMS FOR CLASSROOM REINFORCEMENT

PRACTICE PROBLEM #01
An astronaut capsule is filled with 80°C hot water and placed into a 0°C ice bath. After 15 minutes, its thermometer reads 63.8°C. What is the capsule's insulation decay constant k?
💡 Hint: Extract values: T₀ = 80, T_env = 0, T(15) = 63.8, t = 15. Plug into k = -ln(63.8 / 80) / 15.
✓ Solution: ratio = 63.8/80 = 0.7975. ln(0.7975) = -0.2263. k = -(-0.2263)/15 = 0.015 min⁻¹. This matches the Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI) spacesuit!
PRACTICE PROBLEM #02
If a Mylar-wrapped capsule has k = 0.040 and starts at 80°C in a 0°C ice bath, what will its temperature be after 20 minutes?
💡 Hint: Use T(20) = 0 + (80 - 0) * e^(-0.040 * 20).
✓ Solution: e^(-0.040 * 20) = e^(-0.8) = 0.4493. T(20) = 80 * 0.4493 = 35.9°C.
