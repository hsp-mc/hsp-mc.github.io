WELCOME, MISSION ENGINEERS

You are now logged into the European SPHERE Initiative Mission Control terminal. You have been tasked with designing, modeling, and verifying the thermal performance of our next-generation Extravehicular Mobility Unit (EMU) - the spacesuit worn by astronauts operating outside the ISS.

Outer space is a thermal extreme. In direct sunlight, temperatures soar to +120°C. In the shadow of the Earth, they plunge to -150°C. Without an advanced spacesuit capsule that controls heat conduction and radiation, an astronaut would succumb to thermal shock in minutes.

In this mission, you will simulate a spacesuit environment using a 100ml PET jar filled with 80°C hot water (representing the warm internal cabin of our simulated astronaut). Your capsule will be subjected to the extreme cold of deep space—analogized by a 0°C ice-water bath.

Your Objectives: First, understand the physical setup. Second, model the thermodynamics in our Prediction Simulator. Third, log actual real-world telemetry during the lab drop and correlate your model with the physical curve!

THE CORE SCIENCE OF SPACE SUIT ANALOGS

COTTON SHEETS — CONDUCTION BARRIER

Mechanism: Cotton fiber matrices trap air micro-pockets. Air is a poor heat conductor. The cotton acts as a conduction shield, slowing the transfer of kinetic energy between molecules from the hot PET capsule walls directly to the surrounding ice bath.

BUBBLE WRAP — CONVECTIVE AIR ENVELOPE

Mechanism: Convection requires the circulation of a fluid (liquid or gas) carrying heat. By trapping air in rigid plastic bubbles, bubble wrap locks gas molecules in place. Warm air cannot circulate to colder areas, effectively neutralizing convective thermal losses.

MYLAR FOIL — MULTI-LAYER RADIATION SHIELD (MLI)

Mechanism: Even in a complete vacuum, bodies lose heat via thermal light (infrared radiation). Real spacesuits utilize shiny, silvered Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI). Mylar emergency blankets act as active reflectors, bouncing thermal infrared radiation waves back inside.

CRITICAL CLASSROOM SAFETY PROTOCOLS

Thermodynamic experiments in school environments introduce manageable risks. Ensure the following rules are strictly enforced during the lab drop:

HAZARD DESCRIPTION RISK LAYER MITIGATION MANDATE
Boiling Water Scalding
Using boiling 100°C water directly in school environments.		 HIGH Enforce a strict 80°C limit. Provide thermal kettles pre-programmed to 80°C or use standard thermometers to lock temperatures before filling plastic capsules.
Ice-Bath Spillages

BOARDWORK KEYFRAMES & EQUATIONS MATH_THEORY // DIRECTIVE

Pedagogical Guidance

Use these explanations to anchor class theories on the chalkboard:

1. SPECIFIC HEAT CAPACITY

Calculates total energy loss from internal core volume to surroundings:

Q = m · c · ΔT

Where: m = 0.1 kg (100ml water), c = 4184 J/kg·°C (Specific heat capacity of water), ΔT = Temperature change.

2. NEWTON'S LAW OF COOLING

Models the rate of heat loss proportional to temperature differences:

T(t) = T_env + (T₀ - T_env) · e⁻ᵏᵗ

Where: T₀ = 80°C, T_env = 0°C (ice bath), k = insulation cooling constant, t = time.
