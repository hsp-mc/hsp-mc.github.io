Inquiry-Based Science Education Focus This manual provides a complete hardware assembly guide and digital workflow to construct a physical thermal envelope (an astronaut spacesuit capsule) and map its real cooling dynamics inside a vacuum analog to a live Newtonian mathematical prediction engine.

1. Introduction & The Digital Twin Paradigm

During deep-space voyages, spacecraft and astronauts are subjected to severe thermal extremes. In low Earth orbit (such as on the International Space Station), direct solar irradiation can heat surfaces to 121°C, while objects in orbital shadows radiate energy away, plunging to -157°C. Creating reliable, multi-layer thermal envelopes is critical to preserving astronaut safety.

In this laboratory experiment, you will build a protective "spacesuit" for a simulated astronaut (a 100ml PET container filled with hot water at 80°C). You will drop this unit into an ice-bath chamber (0°C) mimicking the thermal void of space, and use the SPHERE: Mission Control dashboard as your live telemetry terminal.

This project leverages the Digital Twin Ecosystem, aligned with the real-world European Union Horizon 2020 SPHERE initiative. A Digital Twin is a real-time mathematical replication of a physical device. By pairing your physical sensor readings with the simulator's theoretical predictions, you will evaluate heat dissipation efficiency and insulation integrity!

2. Hardware Assembly Guide

To ensure the experiment is economically scalable across diverse classrooms, the hardware relies exclusively on affordable, off-the-shelf industrial and household components:

  • Capsule Body: 1x 100ml wide-mouth PET food jar.
  • Waterproof Routing: 1x Industrial PG7 Nylon Cable Gland (mounted securely through a 12.5mm hole drilled in the PET jar cap).
  • Thermal Sensor: 1x DS18B20 digital waterproof temperature sensor probe.
  • Environmental Chamber: A large tub or bucket filled with crushed ice and water (maintained at 0°C).

Step-by-Step Mechanical Assembly:

  1. Thread the digital DS18B20 sensor wire through the PG7 nylon gland body.
  2. Screw the PG7 gland locknut onto the inside of the jar cap until firmly sealed.
  3. Tighten the external dome nut of the PG7 gland. This compresses the internal neoprene rubber seal around the sensor wire, creating a 100% waterproof seal capable of sustaining submersion in ice water.
  4. Verify that the sensor probe is suspended freely in the middle of the jar and does not directly touch the bottom or sides of the PET capsule.

3. Insulation Schemes & Optimization

Your main engineering challenge is to test different insulation configurations wrapped around the physical capsule to slow the temperature decay:

  • Configuration A (Bare Capsule): No insulation applied. Serves as your scientific control baseline.
  • Configuration B (Cotton Padding): Represents conduction protection. Heavy cotton layers absorb mechanical heat but allow air movement.
  • Configuration C (Bubble Wrap Rings): Represents convection prevention. Captures static air pockets, slowing convective heat transfer.
  • Configuration D (Multi-Layer Insulation - MLI): Reflective aluminum Mylar foil layers separated by thin netting. The gold standard for space flight, designed to block radiant heat transfer.

4. Mathematical Core & Newton's Cooling Formula

The rate at which the warm water capsule cools in the cold ice bath is modeled by Newton's Law of Cooling. This law states that the rate of heat loss of an object is directly proportional to the difference in temperatures between the object and its surrounding environment:

dT/dt = -k · (T - T_env)
differential rate equation

By integrating this differential equation, we get the analytical prediction formula utilized by the SPHERE Mathematical Engine:

T(t) = T_env + (T₀ - T_env) · e⁻ᵏᵗ
integrated exponential prediction model

Where:

  • T(t): The predicted temperature at time t (seconds).
  • T_env: The environmental ice-water temperature (fixed at 0°C).
  • T₀: The initial temperature of the warm water at drop-time (typically 80°C).
  • k: The cooling decay coefficient (1/seconds) which represents the heat transfer rate. A lower k constant indicates a more efficient spacesuit!
  • e: Euler's mathematical constant (~2.71828).

5. Step-by-Step Mission Control Workflow

Follow this precise mission timeline during your laboratory execution. You can **check off each milestone** to keep your crew synchronized in real time:

Milestone 01: Pre-Flight Certification
Read the Mission Briefing on the home page and complete the Pre-Flight Quiz to prove your laboratory readiness and unlock the control terminal.
Milestone 02: Run Newtonian Predictions
Open the Simulator tab. Select your insulation type, configure your initial variables (e.g. T₀ = 80°C, T_env = 0°C), and click RUN SIMULATION PREDICTION to generate the digital twin curve.
Milestone 03: Prepare Physical Asset
Fill the PET capsule with hot water pre-measured at 80°C. Secure the cap and tighten the PG7 gland to prevent convective water leakage.
Milestone 04: Submerge & Initialize
Submerge the physical capsule into the 0°C ice water bath. Simultaneously, click START LIVE TIMER inside the Live Telemetry tab.
Milestone 05: Active Sensor Logging
Every 60 seconds (the alarm terminal will blink green and beep), read the current physical thermometer value and log it. Click RECORD DATA POINT to plot the point.
Milestone 06: Analyze Correlation Score
Once at least 5 points are logged, review your calculated Thermal Correlation Score. Discuss with your team how closely your physical trial tracked the digital model!
Summer & High-Ambient Temperature Protocol (~30°C Classroom) If you are running this experiment in a hot room (e.g. during summer conditions), enforce these key safety adjustments:
  • Safety Temp Scaling (Mitigate Scalding Risk): Scale the initial temperature down from 80°C to 55°C–60°C. Newton's Law of Cooling works identically! Enter T₀ = 60°C in your simulator prediction, and fill your physical jar with 60°C water.
  • Melting Ice Compensation: On hot days, your ice bath water may rise slightly above 0°C. Measure the actual temperature of your ice bath at the start, and enter that exact value as the environmental temperature T_env in your Simulator tab.
  • Ice-Free Tap Water Drop: If ice is not logistically available, submerge your capsule in room-temperature tap water. Simply measure the tap water's temperature, log it as T_env, and run your predictions!
